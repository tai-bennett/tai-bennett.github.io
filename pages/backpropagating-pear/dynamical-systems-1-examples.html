When studying any field of mathematics, it is often a good idea to have some "toy" examples for each major concept in the area. We proceed with some examples from simple to more complicated.

1. Maps with stable asymptotic behavior

One major tool in dynamical systems that is used very often in different contexts is the contraction mapping principle. It is often applied to certain maps in a functional space associated to the dynamical system in questions and not to the original system.

Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space and \(f: X \to X\) a map. We call \(f\) contracting if there exists \(\lambda < 1\) such that

\begin{align*} d(f(x), f(y)) \leq \lambda d(x,y) && x,y \in X. \end{align*}

Given a contracting map \(f\), if we consider the $n$-th iterate \(f^n\) we can see that any two arbitrary points with get arbitrarily close. It can be shown that any sequence of iterates \(y_n = f^n(x)\) will converge exponentially fast.

Proposition: (Contraction Mapping Principle) Let \(X\) be a complete metric space. Under the action of iterates of a contracting map \(f: X \to X\) all points converge with exponential speed to a unique fixed point of \(f\).

Thus for a contracting map all points are asymptotic to a unique fixed point. This result will often be used in the course of our study of dynamical systems with more complicated behavior.

Another important idea is that of continuation. That is, a small perturbation in the dynamical system shouldn't change certain behaviors. This is a major idea in topological dynamical systems. A very "down to earth" example of this is the follow proposition.

Proposition: If \(p\) is a periodic point of period \(m\) for a \(C^1\) map \(f\) and the differential \(Df^m_p\) does not have \(1\) as an eigenvalue then for every map \(g\) sufficiently close to \(f\) in the \(C^1\) topology there exists a unique periodic point of period \(m\) close to \(p\).

In particular, the fixed point \(p\) of a contraction \(f\) should be close to the fixed point \(q\) of a contraction \(g\) with \(f\) and \(g\) are close in the \(C^1\) topology.

2. Linear Maps

Like in many other fields, it is often useful to linearize locally and to leverage a strong theory on linear objects to the nonlinear case. This is also true in dynamical system. A rich theory exists for a special case of linear dynamical systems.

Definition: A linear map on \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is called hyperbolic if all of its eigenvalues have absolute value different from 1.

In the case of hyperbolic dynamical systems, we get that the local behavior around the origin tells of everything about the global behavior.

The spectrum of the linear map can also tells us a lot about the dynamical system. Consider \(E_{\lambda}\) to be the space of generalized eigenvectors of \(\lambda\) and \(E_{\lambda, \bar{\lambda}}\) to be that of a complex eigenvalue and its conjugate. Then the underlying vector space (\(\mathbb{R}^n\) for us) can be decomposed into

\begin{align*} \mathbb{R}^n &= E^- \oplus E^+ \oplus E^0\\ E^- &= \bigoplus_{|lambda|<1} E_{\lambda} \oplus \bigoplus_{|\lambda|<1} E_{\lambda, \bar{\lambda}}\\ E^+ &= \bigoplus_{|lambda|>1} E_{\lambda} \oplus \bigoplus_{|\lambda|>1} E_{\lambda, \bar{\lambda}}\\ E^0 &= E_1 \oplus E_{-1} \oplus \bigoplus_{|\lambda|=1} E_{\lambda, \bar{\lambda}}. \end{align*}

We call \(E^-\) and \(E^+\) the contracting and expanding subspaces respectively. This will later extend to the ideas of stable and unstable manifolds. Another way to define hyperbolicity of a linear dynamical system is by triviality of \(E^0\) (that is \(\R^n = E^- \oplus E^+\)).

3. Rotations of the circle

In the case when a linear dynamical systems consists of only complex eigenvalues, you may know that the transformation purely rotates the space. This leads naturally into study dynamical systems on the cirle (and tori). Maps of rotations on the torus are a great toy example for the topological ideas of transitivity and minimality.

Definition: A topological dynamical system \(f: X \to X\) is called topologically transitive if there exists a point \(x \in X\) such that its orbit \(\mathcal{O}_f(x) := \{f^n(x)\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}\) is dense in \(X\).

This transitivity means that this sequence/orbit explores all of the phase/state space in a topological sense. (This can also be phrased in terms of generic sets???).

Definition: A topological dynamical system \(f : X \to X\) is called minimal if the orbit of every point \(x \in X\) is dense in \(x\). Equivalently, \(f\) has no proper closed invariant sets.

Example: Let \(R_{\alpha} : S^1 \to S^1\) be the rotation by the angle \(\alpha\). This can be written as \(R_{\alpha}(z) = e^{2\pi i\alpha} z\). Then the map \(R_{\alpha}\) is minimal if and only if \(\alpha\) is irrational. Note that in this case, we can think of minimality and periodicity as opposite ideas.

4. Translations on the torus

We can take the ideas of the last section and apply them to higher dimensional tori. These examples are important in studying completely integrable Hamiltonian systems. One difference that we will see is that minimality and periodicity are not alternatives to each other.

Let \(T_{\lambda} : \mathbb{T}^n \to \mathbb{T}^n\) be the translation map by \(\gamma\) on the $n$-dimensional torus. That is \(T_{\gamma}(x_1, \ldots , x_n) = (x_1 + \gamma_1, \ldots, x_n + \gamma_n) \text{ mod} 1\).

Proposition: The translation \(T_{\gamma}\) is minimal if and only if the numbers \(\gamma_i\) for \(i = 1, \ldots n\) are rationally independent (no integer sum of \(\gamma_i\)'s in an integer).

Note that now we could have submanifolds (circles) of the torus where the limit behavior exists, but on those submanifolds the dynamical system is minimal.

5. Gradient flows

Definition: A point \(y \in X\) is called an $ω$-limit point (or and $α$-limit point for a point \(x \in X\) if there exists a sequence of moments of time going to \(+ \infty\) (or \(- \infty\)) such that the images of \(x\) converge to \(y\). The set of all \(\omega\) limit points for \(x\), denoted \(\omega(x)\), is called the \(\omega\) limit set. Similarly for \(\alpha\).

\begin{align*} \omega(x) &= \bigcup_{T=0}^{\infty} \overline{\left ( \bigcup_{t \geq T \varphi^t(x) \right )}, \alpha(x) &= \bigcup_{T=0}^{- \infty} \overline{\left ( \bigcup_{t \leq T \varphi^t(x) \right )}, \end{align*}

Thus if \(x\) is compact, the sets \(\omega(x)\) and \(\alpha(x)\) are nonempty and every point sooner or later comes to any given neighborhood of its $ω$-limit set and stays there.

Let \(F\) be and function and consider the gradient flow of \(F\).

Proposition: The set \(\omega_F(x)\) and \(\alpha_F(x)\) consist of critical points of \(F\), that is fixed points of the gradient flow.

Proposition: For any \(x \in M\) and any \(F\) the set \(\omega_F(x)\) is either a single point or an infinite set.

6. Expanding Maps

Let \(E_2(x) = 2x \text{ mod} 1\) be the angle doubling map on the circle.

Definition: For a transformation \(f: X \to X\) we let \(P_n(f)\) be the number of periodic points of \(f\) with (not necessarily minimal) period \(n\). That is, the number of fixed points of \(f^n\).

Proposition: \(P_n(E_2) = 2^n-1\), periodic points for \(E_2\) are dense in \(S^1\), and \(E_2\) is topologically transitive.

Proposition: There exists a point \(x \in S^1\) such that in addition notation, its $ω$-limit set with respect to the map \(E_3\) is that standard middle-third Cantor set \(K\). In particular, \(K\) is $E3$-invariant and contains a dense orbit.
