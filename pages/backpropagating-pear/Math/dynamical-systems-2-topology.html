In this section, we are going to look into the problem of studying the asymptotic behavior of smooth dynamical systems from a different angle. We start with the equivalence classes we should consider in dynamical systems (conjugacy classes) and use those to make some classifications. Then we look more in depth at the topological perspective. In particular, we show some examples of moduli which are local topological invariants of dynamical systems which can sometimes extend to entire orbits.

1. Smooth conjugacy and moduli for maps

Definition: Two \(C^r\) maps \(f: M \to M\) and \(g: N \to N\) are said to be \(C^m\)-equivalent or \(C^m\)-conjugate if there exists a \(C^m\) diffeomorphism \(h : M \to N\) such that \(f = h^{-1} \circ g \circ h\). We call \(h\) the conjugacy.

This definition basically measn that we wish to look at dynamical systems up to a change of coordinates.

Recall, the basis of the \(C^r\) topology consists of neighborhoods

\begin{align*} \mathcal{N}^r(f; \Phi, \Psi, K, \varepsilon) \end{align*}

that contain maps \(g \in C^r(M,M)\) such that

\begin{align*} \| D^k(\psi_i f \varphi_i^{-1})(x) - D^k(\psi_i g \varphi_i^{-1})(x)\| < \varepsilon_i \end{align*}

Intuitively, we are making sure that all derivatives are close to each other.

Definition: Let \(U\) be an open subset in the space \(C^r(M,M)\) of \(C^r\) maps of \(M\) into itself with the \(C^r\) topology. A continuous function \(F: U \to \mathbb{R}\) is called a \(C^m\) modulus if there exists a \(\delta > 0\) such that \(F(f) = F(g)\) for any two maps \(f,g \in U\) that are \(C^m\) equivalent via a diffeomorphism \(h\) with \(d_{C^m}(h, id) < \delta\).

Next, we show some examples of moduli. One example of a modulus is a mapping to the spectrum of periodic points. Let \(f\) have a periodic point \(p\). Let \(g\) be a conjugate of \(f\) by \(h\). Then \(g\) also has \(p\) as a periodic point. Suppose that \(h\) is a \(C^m\) equivalence.

\begin{align*} Df_x^n = Dh^{-1}_{g^nhx} \cird Dg^n_{hx} \circ Dh_x \end{align*}

and in particular if $fnp = p we have

\begin{align*} Df^n_p = (Dh_p)^{-1} Dg^n_{hp} Dh_p \end{align*}

Then the operators \(Df^n_p\) and \(Dg^n_{hp}\) are conjugate and have the same spectrum. Then, for example, the mapping \(F_i : f \mapsto \sigma(Df^n_p)_i\) is well defined on conjugacy classes. Since this is true for all conjugacies \(h\) then our \(\delta\) can be arbitrary.

Consider \(f \in C^1(M,M)\). Then consider a periodic point \(p\) of \(f\) whose spectrum does not contain \(1\). Let \(U\) be a neighborhood of \(f\) such that each \(g \in U\) so that \(g\) has a unique periodic point (of the same period) close to \(p\). Then define \(F: U \to \mathbb{R}\) to map the function to the largest eigenvalue at the periodic point. That is

\begin{align*} F(g) &= \lambda_1 \in \sigma(D^n_q). \end{align*}

2. Topological conjugacy, factors and structural stability

Definition: For \(r \geq 0\) two \(C^r\) maps \(f: M \to M\) and \(g: N \to N\) are said to be topologically conjugate if there exists a homeomorphism \(h : M \to N\) such that \(f = h^{-1} \circ g \circ h\).

Definition: A map \(g: N \to N\) is a factor (or topological factor) of \(f : M \to M\) if there exists a surjective continuous map \(h : M \to N\) such that \(h \circ f = g \circ h\). The map \(h\) is called a semiconjugacy.

The following two definitions can also be extended to flows in a natural way.

Definition: A \(C^r\) map \(f\) is \(C^m\) structurally stable (\(1 \leq m \leq r\)) if there exists a neighborhood \(U\) of \(f\) in the \(C^m\) topology such that every map \(g \in U\) is topologically conjugate to \(f\).

Definition: A \(C^r\) map is \(C^m\) strongly structurally stable if it is structurally stable and in addition for any \(g \in U\) one can choose a conjugating homeomorphism \(h = h_g\) in such a way that both \(h_g\) and \(h_g^{-1}\) uniformly converge to the identity as \(g\) converges to \(f\) in \(C^m\) topology.

3. Topological classification of expanding maps on a circle

Defintion: A continuous map \(f : X \to X\) is called expanding if form some \(\mu > 1\), *ε0 > 0$ and every \(x,y \in X\) with \(x \neq y\) and \(d(x,y) < \varepsilon_0\) one has

\begin{align*} d(f(x), f(y)) > \mu d(x,y). \end{align*}

The maps \(E_m : S^1 \to S^1\), \(|m| \geq 2\),

\begin{align*} E_m(z) = z^m \end{align*}

are the toy examples of expanding maps on the circle. One can also extend these maps to high dimensional tori. For example, \(E_m \times E_k\). In addition, any matrix

\begin{align*} L = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \end{align*}

with \(a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{Z}\) and \(\sigma(L) \subset \mathbb{C} \setminus B_1(0)\) can be projected down to the torus and form an expanding map

\begin{align*} F_L(x,y) = (ax + by, cx + dy) (\text{mod } 1). \end{align*}

This also generalizes to higher dimensional tori.

Proposition: If \(M\) is a compact Riemannian manifold and \(f\) is a differentiable map such that there exists \(\mu > 1\) satisfying

\begin{align*} \| D_x fv \| > \mu \| v \| \end{align*}

for any \(v \in T_xM \setminus \{0\}\). Then \(f\) is an expanding map.

Definition: If \(f: S^1 \to S^1\) and \(F\) is any life of \(f\) then \(F(x+1) - F(x)\) is called the degree of \(f\) and denoted by deg\((f)\). Recall that a life is a map \(F: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) such that

\begin{align*} f \circ \pi = \pi \circ F \end{align*}

where \(\pi : \mathbb{R} \to S^1\) is the universal covering of \(S^1 = \mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}\).

It can be shown that the degree is continuous and thus locally constant in the \(C^0\) uniform topology.

Theorem: Everying expanding map \(f\) of the circle of degree \(k\) is toplogically conjugate to the map \(E_k\). Furthermore, if \(f\) is close enough to \(E_k\) in the \(C^0\) topology, then the conjugating homeomorphism can be chose close to the identity.

4. Coding, horseshoes and Markov partitions

Semiconjugacies between one-sided shifts and expand- ing maps of the circle represent a simple but very effective example of coding of orbits for a smooth system, a concept alluded to at the beginning of Section 1.9. In general one can try to code orbits of, say, a diffeomorphism, or even a homeomorphism f, by dividing its phase space \(X\) into finitely many pieces \(X_0, \ldots , X_{N - 1}\) and registering to which of the pieces successive iterates of a point \(x \in X\) belong. Two basic difficulties appear in this construction:

\begin{enumerate} \item If the pieces overlap, then each point does not have a unique sequence representation. \item all points in the intersection \begin{align*} \bigcap_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} f^{-n} (X_{\omega_n}) \end{align*} are coded by the same sequence $\omega = \{\omega_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$. \end{enumerate}
